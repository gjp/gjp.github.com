Project: ALDImeals.com

This site was launched December 2008 and was decommissioned at the end of a multi-year promotion, at which point it had over 100k registered users. A video review, some nice press, and snapshots from the Internet Archive may still be found online.

The project posed some interesting challenges, as the codebase I'd been working with until this point was a Frankenstein's monster consisting of proxied Rails apps bolted on to a legacy CGI system consisting of dozens of Perl scripts and approximately 8k lines of C code. In 2008. Also, the client requested a drag-and-drop UI at a time before jQueryUI was mature, and the majority of ALDI's customers still used IE6.

I was the dev team, the database guy, the sysadmin, and I'd even built the box the site was intended to run on, so I made an executive decision to break with the past and port everything to Rails.

No direct interaction was allowed with the client – I worked with two different marketing companies. The core design was provided by a single Photoshop mockup, seen here.

Near pixel-accurate layout was required. Fixed-sized boxes enclosed dynamic content of varying length. Nothing on the pages was allowed to flow. Given the constraints, tables would have been the easy route here (perhaps the sane one). But I really did not want to use tables.

I adjusted colors to reflect the palette used on the main ALDI US site and padded dynamic content to help avoid layout breakage. The resulting design (captured from the production site) was actually quite faithful to the mockup. And no tables!

Various fragments of the page were cached in memcached with differing timeouts ranging from 5 minutes to an hour, allowing for low overhead on a fairly busy page containing multiple dynamic elements and rotating banners.

A core feature of the site was the drag-and-drop Meal Planner. The final design used the entire site width to include three different elements: A tabbed cookbook, including both ALDI- and user-selected recipes, a calendar spanning one week, and a concise shopping list. Recipe titles could be dragged from the cookbook to either the calendar or shopping list, and from the calendar to the shopping list.

The guts of the application have since been ported to several other sites based on the Radiant CMS as well as a web service which is fronted by a .NET-based e-commerce package.
